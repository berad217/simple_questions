# Section 9: Self-Advocacy Toolkit

## What This Section Is For

This section is for **you**—the person with the high-uncertainty cognitive style.

It solves the problem: *"How do I get people to use these techniques without handing them a 50-page manual?"*

You don't need your boss or partner to read this entire document. You just need a few scripts to explain how your brain works and how to get the inputs you need to function at your best.

After reading this, you should be able to:

- Explain your cognitive style as a "high-resolution processor," not a defect.
- Request specific question structures in real-time without using jargon.
- Defend your need for processing time without sounding like you're stalling.
- Manage your energy so you don't burn out from "forced collapsing."

---

## 1. The Explanation Script

You need a way to explain this to new bosses, teams, or partners *before* a crisis happens. The goal is to frame it as an **input requirement**, not a personality quirk.

**The "High-Resolution" Metaphor**
> "I have a 'high-resolution' processing style. When you ask a question, I don't just see the answer; I see the dependencies, the risks, and the edge cases. That makes me great at spotting problems early, but it means I sometimes freeze on simple questions because I see five different ways to answer them."

**Script for a New Boss**
> "I work best when questions have clear constraints. If you ask 'How's the project?', I might struggle because I'm analyzing the timeline, the budget, and the team dynamics all at once. If you ask 'Logistically, are we on track?', I can give you an immediate answer. The more specific you are about *which* part of the project you want to know about, the faster I can answer."

**Script for a Partner**
> "Sometimes when I pause after you ask me something, it's not because I'm ignoring you or hiding something. It's because the question triggered a huge map in my head and I'm trying to figure out which part you care about. If you see me staring blankly, I'm not checking out—I'm processing. It helps if you tell me what kind of answer you need."

---

## 2. Real-Time Restructuring Requests

You can't expect everyone to learn terms like "Domain-Binding" or "Forced-Slice." But you can ask for the *effect* of those techniques using normal language.

| If you need... | Don't say... | Say this... |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Domain-Binding** | "Please bind the domain." | "Are you asking logistically, or about the feelings involved?" |
| **Uncertainty Permission** | "I need uncertainty permission." | "I can give you a rough guess now, or a precise answer tomorrow. Which do you prefer?" |
| **Forced-Slice** | "I am overwhelmed by dimensionality." | "There are 5 or 6 factors here. Do you want the biggest one, or the full list?" |
| **Intent Declaration** | "Declare your intent." | "Just to make sure I give you the right level of detail—is this for a quick update, or a deep dive?" |
| **Role-Locking** | "Lock my role." | "Do you want my answer as your friend, or my answer as a lawyer?" |

**The "Help Me Help You" Frame**
Always frame these requests as "I want to give you a good answer," not "Your question is bad."
- *Bad*: "That's too vague. Be specific."
- *Good*: "I want to give you exactly what you need—are you looking for the timeline or the budget?"

---

## 3. Reframing "Slow" as "Deep"

When you pause to map the dimensions, it can look like stalling. You need to narrate the process so it looks like **competence**, not confusion.

**The "Mapping" Narrative**
Instead of saying "Uhh..." or "I don't know," say:
> "I'm just mapping the dependencies on that... okay, I see three risks..."

**The "Constraint Check" Narrative**
> "I'm running that against our budget constraints... give me a second..."

**Why this works**: It tells the other person that silence = work. You are actively computing, not passively failing.

---

## 4. Energy Management: The "Amputation Budget"

Forcing yourself to collapse a multi-dimensional map into a simple answer ("Just say yes or no!") takes cognitive energy. It is active work.

If you do this all day, you will burn out. This is why you might be brilliant at work (where you force-collapse everything) and then unable to decide on dinner (because your budget is empty).

**Strategies**:

1. **Identify High-Cost People**: Who consistently asks broad, unbounded questions? Prepare "pre-collapsed" updates for them.
2. **Declare "Low Res" Days**: Tell your partner, "My decision fatigue is high today. I need simple, binary choices tonight."
3. **Don't Fake It When You Can't**: If you don't have the energy to collapse the map, use the **Uncertainty Permission** script: "I'm too fried to give you a definitive answer right now. Current gut check is X, but don't hold me to it."

---

## Summary

You are not broken. You just have a high-bandwidth input requirement.

1. **Tell them**: "I see the details. Help me focus."
2. **Ask them**: "Do you want the fast answer or the precise one?"
3. **Show them**: Narrate your processing ("I'm checking the risks...") so they see the value.
