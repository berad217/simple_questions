# High-Uncertainty Cognitive Style & Communication Techniques
## Original Genesis Document (Nikki's Summary)

---

## 1. Brief Description of the Cognitive Style

Some highly intelligent minds are organized less around closure and more around epistemic openness. These systems continuously generate and evaluate multiple interpretations, consequences, and missing-information pathways. Rather than producing stable "positions," they naturally produce landscapes of possibilities.

**Key characteristic**: Commitment to a single answer feels inaccurate, risky, or dishonest because it amputates uncertainty.

### Strengths
- Deep modeling
- Systems thinking
- Diagnostic ability
- Sensitivity to downstream effects

### Difficulties
Environments that require fast collapse into stable statements:
- Court testimony
- Leadership decisions
- Emotional conversations
- Relational reassurance

In these contexts, the person may externally verbalize ongoing evaluation rather than delivering a settled answer.

---

## 2. Core Communication Problem

Open questions are automatically expanded into high-dimensional problems involving causal, emotional, moral, strategic, and reputional variables at once. The cognitive system attempts to answer all layers simultaneously, resulting in hedging, over-qualification, or apparent non-answers.

**The solution is not "simpler thinking," but structured collapse.**

---

## 3. Techniques for Asking Easier-to-Answer Questions

### Domain-Binding
Restrict which layer of reality is being queried.

**Example:**
- Instead of: "Why didn't you call?"
- Use: "Logistically only — what stopped you from calling yesterday?"

### Uncertainty Permission
Explicitly allow provisional or incomplete answers.

**Example:**
- "Given what you knew at the time, even if it was wrong, what did you think was happening?"

### Forced-Slice Questions
Ask for one factor rather than the full explanation set.

**Example:**
- "If you had to name just one factor, what mattered most?"

### Counterfactual Anchoring
Ask what difference would have changed the outcome.

**Example:**
- "What would have needed to be different for you to say yes?"

### Output-Format Constraints
Define the structure of the answer before it's given.

**Examples:**
- "Yes or no first, explanation after."
- "One sentence, even if incomplete."

### Role-Locking
Specify which internal role should answer.

**Examples:**
- "Answer this as a manager, not as a theorist."
- "Answer as if you had to act today."

---

## 4. Practical Summary

These techniques do not reduce intelligence or nuance. **They reduce dimensionality.**

They tell the cognitive system which uncertainties may be temporarily set aside so that a usable position can form.

**The goal is not perfect truth, but communicable stability.**
