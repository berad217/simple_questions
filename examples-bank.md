### Uncertainty Permission

**Canonical Example**:
- **Instead of**: "What did you think was happening?"
- **Use**: "Given what you knew at the time, even if it was wrong, what did you think was happening?"

**Technique Demonstrated**: Uncertainty permission (explicitly allows provisional/incomplete answers)

**Used In**:
- genesis_document.md (original source)
- [To be expanded in Section 4.1]

**Notes**: The phrase "even if it was wrong" is key—it gives explicit permission to be uncertain/incorrect.

### Forced-Slice Questions

**Canonical Example**:
- **Instead of**: "What mattered in that decision?"
- **Use**: "If you had to name just one factor, what mattered most?"

**Technique Demonstrated**: Forced-slice (asks for one factor rather than full explanation set)

**Used In**:
- genesis_document.md (original source)
- [To be expanded in Section 4.1]

**Notes**: "If you had to name just one" does the forcing. "Mattered most" asks for prioritization, not comprehensiveness.

---

### Counterfactual Anchoring

**Canonical Example**:
- **Instead of**: "Why did you say no?"
- **Use**: "What would have needed to be different for you to say yes?"

**Technique Demonstrated**: Counterfactual anchoring (asks what difference would have changed outcome)

**Used In**:
- genesis_document.md (original source)
- [To be expanded in Section 4.1]

**Notes**: This flips from "explain past decision" to "identify the delta that would change it"—often much easier to answer.

## Daily Life Friction Examples (from Section 1)

### "How was your weekend?"

**Context**: Casual social question expecting brief answer

**Friction Pattern**: High-uncertainty individual gives extended response with caveats and alternative framings

**What it looks like**: Over-sharing. Not reading social cues. Making simple things complicated.

**What's actually happening**: Person is attempting to transmit accurate representation of experience that doesn't fit neatly into "good" or "bad."

**Used In**:
- section-01-core-cognitive-profile.md (How This Presents in Daily Life)

---

### "Do you love me?"

**Context**: Intimate relationship, emotional reassurance needed

**Friction Pattern**: Question has a "correct answer" but high-uncertainty individual sees complexity in types/contexts of love

**The Problem**: The information exchange serves a relationship function that complexity undermines. Partner wants "yes" with conviction, not seventeen different ways love manifests.

**Used In**:
- section-01-core-cognitive-profile.md (When Friction Occurs - emotional reassurance)

---

### "What do you want for dinner?"

**Context**: Casual domestic decision-making requiring speed

**Friction Pattern**: Simple logistical question triggers cognitive expansion, creating awkward pause

**The Problem**: Speed contexts don't reward careful analysis. Social flow matters more than precision.

**Used In**:
- section-01-core-cognitive-profile.md (When Friction Occurs - speed)

## Three-Layer Communication Model (Canonical Formulation)

**The Three Layers** (from onboarding.md):
1. **Literal information** - the explicit content
2. **Relationship signal** - what this exchange means about trust, priority, care
3. **Common knowledge** - what both parties now know they both know

**Key Insight**: High-uncertainty individuals often over-optimize for layer 1 while underweighting layers 2 and 3.

**Used In**:
- onboarding.md (Key Concepts section)
- [To appear in Section 3.3: Game Theory & Signaling Perspective]
- [To appear in Section 7.1: Dual-Purpose Problem]

**Notes**: This formulation should remain consistent. The "what both parties now know they both know" phrasing for layer 3 is important—it captures the recursive/meta nature of common knowledge.

4. **Note variations**: Context-specific adaptations (professional/intimate/casual)
5. **Explain key elements**: What makes this example work

When using an example from this bank in a section:
1. **Check bank first**: Use canonical formulation
2. **Update "Used In"**: Add section reference
3. **Flag contradictions**: If you need to use it differently, note why in DEVLOG.md
